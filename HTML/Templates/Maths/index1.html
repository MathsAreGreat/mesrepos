The Pythagorean Theorem

Exploring the Fundamental Relationship in Right Triangles

Introduction

The Pythagorean Theorem is one of the most fundamental and well-known mathematical principles, dating back to ancient times. Named after the Greek mathematician Pythagoras, this theorem establishes a crucial relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle.

This theorem has applications across geometry, trigonometry, physics, engineering, and many other fields.

The Theorem

In any right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

c² = a² + b²

Where:

  • c is the length of the hypotenuse
  • a and b are the lengths of the other two sides

Visual Representation

a b c

The theorem can be visualized by the squares constructed on each side of the triangle. The area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum of the areas of the squares on the other two sides.

Algebraic Proof

One of the simplest proofs involves rearranging four identical right triangles within a larger square:

  1. Consider four identical right triangles with sides a and b, and hypotenuse c.
  2. Arrange them within a larger square of side length (a + b).
  3. The area of the larger square is (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b².
  4. The area can also be calculated as the sum of the four triangles and the smaller square: 4 × (½ab) + c² = 2ab + c².
  5. Setting these equal: a² + 2ab + b² = 2ab + c².
  6. Subtract 2ab from both sides to obtain: a² + b² = c².

Practical Applications

Distance Calculation

The theorem is used to calculate distances between points in coordinate geometry. The distance between (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) is √((x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²).

Construction

Builders use the 3-4-5 rule (a special case of the theorem) to ensure perfect right angles in construction projects.

Navigation

GPS systems and other navigation tools use the theorem to calculate shortest paths and distances.

Physics

The theorem appears in vector mathematics, calculating resultants of perpendicular forces and components.
